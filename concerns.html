Concerns

Safety

Drones have massive safety concerns. Drones typically are lightweight, to make it more easy to fly. But the drones fly at a high height, and if any of the drone’s systems go offline or become damaged, the drone becomes a projectile hurtling towards the ground. In fact, in September 2013, a man’s own drone decapitated him while he was flying it in Brooklyn Park. Another safety concern regarding drones involves air traffic. Between March 2014 and December 2014, the FAA reported close to 200 incidents involving drones navigating alarmingly close to manned aircraft. The FAA does require that drones be flown under 400 feet, but the chance of interfering with manned traffic is still present and presents a very distinct risk.

Privacy

There are many privacy concerns associated with drones, and their ability to capture with less restrictions than a human photographer is the biggest reason for that. These devices can cause privacy intrusions and take pictures of people’s personal lives. There are rules to prevent the drones from capturing pictures inside or near houses; in 1946, the U.S Supreme Court decided in the case U.S. v Causby that homeowner property rights extended only 83 feet above the ground. This prevents low-flying drones from taking private pictures, but doesn’t prevent drones above 83 feet using a higher resolution and more powerful camera. The potential for drones to open otherwise closed doors has been the concern of many.
